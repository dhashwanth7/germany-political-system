Slide 03 — Historical Background

Forged by
History

Germany's current democratic system emerged from the ashes of World War II and decades of division, shaped by painful lessons from the Weimar Republic and Nazi dictatorship.

1919
Weimar Republic
Germany's first democracy — fragile, plagued by economic crisis and political extremism, ultimately collapsing in 1933.
1933–1945
Nazi Dictatorship
Hitler's totalitarian regime dismantled democratic institutions. The Holocaust and WWII left Germany devastated and divided.
1949
Federal Republic Founded
The Grundgesetz (Basic Law) is adopted. West Germany establishes a stable parliamentary democracy with strong safeguards against authoritarianism.
1990
Reunification
East and West Germany reunite on October 3. The Basic Law extends to all 16 states, forming the modern Federal Republic of Germany.
Slide 04 — Structure of Government

Three Branches of
Government

Germany's government is divided into three branches, each with distinct powers and checks on the others to prevent concentration of authority.

⚖️
Legislative
The Bundestag (lower house) and Bundesrat (upper house of states) make federal laws. The Bundestag is directly elected by citizens every 4 years.
🏛️
Executive
The Federal Chancellor (Bundeskanzler) leads the government and holds real executive power. The Federal President is the head of state with a ceremonial role.
🔏
Judicial
The Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) is the guardian of the Basic Law. It can strike down laws that violate the constitution.
Slide 05 — Key Constitutional Organs

The Five Constitutional
Organs

Germany's Basic Law establishes five supreme constitutional bodies that form the backbone of the federal state.

Bundestag
The federal parliament — 736 directly elected members. Passes laws, elects the Chancellor, and controls the budget.
Bundesrat
Represents the 16 Länder (states). Participates in federal legislation, especially laws affecting the states.
Federal President
Head of state, elected for 5 years by the Federal Assembly. Mainly ceremonial — signs laws, represents Germany abroad.
Federal Government
Led by the Chancellor, who sets policy guidelines. Ministers run individual departments. The Chancellor cannot be removed without a constructive vote of no confidence.
Federal Court
The Bundesverfassungsgericht interprets the Basic Law. Its rulings are final and binding on all state powers.
Slide 06 — Political Parties

The Major
Political Parties

Germany's multiparty system encourages coalition governments. Multiple parties compete for seats in the Bundestag.

CDU/CSU
Christian Democratic Union / Christian Social Union
Centre-right. The dominant force in German politics for much of the post-war era. Angela Merkel served 16 years as Chancellor.
SPD
Social Democratic Party
Centre-left. One of Germany's oldest parties. Focuses on social justice, workers' rights, and the welfare state.
Greens
Bündnis 90/Die Grünen
Green politics, environmentalism, and progressive social values. Coalition partner in many recent governments.
FDP
Free Democratic Party
Classical liberal. Pro-market, civil liberties, and individual freedoms. Frequently a coalition kingmaker.
AfD
Alternative für Deutschland
Right-wing populist party founded in 2013. Entered Bundestag in 2017, opposition party known for nationalist positions.
Die Linke
The Left
Democratic socialist party. Advocates redistribution, pacifism, and strong social safety nets. Successor to East German SED.
Slide 07 — Electoral System

How Germany
Votes

Germany uses a unique mixed-member proportional representation system that combines direct constituency voting with party list votes.

"Every German citizen gets two votes — one for a local candidate, one for a party."
The Two-Vote System
🗳️
First Vote (Erststimme)
Voters choose a direct candidate from their constituency. The winner takes a direct seat in the Bundestag — winner takes all in each of 299 constituencies.
📋
Second Vote (Zweitstimme)
The more important vote — cast for a party's regional list. Determines the overall proportion of seats each party receives in the Bundestag.
📊
5% Threshold
Parties must receive at least 5% of second votes (or win 3 direct seats) to enter parliament — preventing extreme fragmentation.
Slide 08 — Federalism

The Federal
Structure

Germany is a federal state — power is deliberately distributed between the central (Bund) government and 16 semi-autonomous states (Länder). This prevents over-centralisation of power.

🏛️
Federal (Bund) Powers
Defence, foreign policy, immigration, currency, and federal infrastructure fall under Bund jurisdiction.
🗺️
State (Länder) Powers
Education, police, local government, cultural affairs, and broadcasting are primarily handled by the 16 Länder.
🤝
Shared Powers
Areas like economic law, housing, and environment are shared — the Bundesrat ensures states have a voice in federal legislation.
⚖️
Why Federalism?
A deliberate response to Nazi centralism — distributing power prevents any single entity from dominating the entire state.
Slide 09 — The Basic Law

The Grundgesetz
— Germany's Constitution

The Grundgesetz (Basic Law), adopted on May 23, 1949, is the supreme law of Germany. Written after WWII, it was designed to prevent the rise of authoritarianism, protecting human dignity as its first and inviolable article.

Human Dignity
Article 1: "Human dignity is inviolable." The most protected principle — even the Bundestag cannot change it.
Democracy
Germany is a democratic republic. All state authority derives from the people through elections and votes.
Federalism
The federal structure distributes power between national and state governments, enshrined permanently in the Basic Law.
Rule of Law
Rechtsstaat — all government action must be grounded in law. No one is above the law, including the government.
Social State
Germany is committed to a welfare state that ensures social security, health, and basic needs for all citizens.
"The free democratic basic order cannot be abolished — even by a majority vote."
Eternity Clause, Article 79(3), Grundgesetz
